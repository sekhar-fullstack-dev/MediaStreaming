Find the dimensional formulae of the following quantities :<br>                 <br>                 (a) the universal constant of gravitation G,<br>                 <br>                 (b) the surface tension $S$,<br>                 <br>                 (c) the thermal conductivity $k$ and<br>                 <br>                 (d) the coefficient of viscosity $\\eta$.<br>                 <br>                 Some equations involving these quantities are<br>                 <br>                 $$<br>                 \\begin{array}{ll}<br>                 F=\\frac{G m_{1} m_{2}}{r^{2}}, \\quad S=\\frac{\\rho g r h}{2}, \\\\<br>                 Q=k \\frac{A\\left(\\theta_{2}-\\theta_{1}\\right) t}{d} \\quad \\text { and } \\quad F=-\\eta A \\frac{v_{2}-v_{1}}{x_{2}-x_{1}}<br>                 \\end{array}<br>                 $$<br>                 <br>                 where the symbols have their usual meanings.<br>                 <br>                 Solution : (a) $F=G \\frac{m_{1} m_{2}}{r^{2}}$<br>                 <br>                 $$<br>                 \\begin{array}{rlrl}<br>                 \\text { or, } & G & =\\frac{F r^{2}}{m_{1} m_{2}} \\\\<br>                 \\text { or, } & {[G]} & =\\frac{[\\mathrm{F}] \\mathrm{L}^{2}}{\\mathrm{M}^{2}}=\\frac{\\mathrm{MLT}^{-2} \\cdot \\mathrm{L}^{2}}{\\mathrm{M}^{2}}=\\mathrm{M}^{-1} \\mathrm{~L}^{3} \\mathrm{~T}^{-2} . \\\\<br>                 \\text { (b) } & & S & =\\frac{\\rho g r h}{2} \\\\<br>                 \\text { or, } & {[S]} & =[\\rho][g] \\mathrm{L}^{2}=\\frac{\\mathrm{M}}{\\mathrm{L}^{3}} \\cdot \\frac{\\mathrm{L}}{\\mathrm{T}^{2}} \\cdot \\mathrm{L}^{2}=\\mathrm{MT}^{-2} . \\\\<br>                 \\text { (c) } & Q & =k \\frac{A\\left(\\theta_{2}-\\theta_{1}\\right) t}{d} \\\\<br>                 \\text { or, } & k & =\\frac{Q d}{A\\left(\\theta_{2}-\\theta_{1}\\right) t} .<br>                 \\end{array}<br>                 $$<br>                 <br>                 Here, $Q$ is the heat energy having dimension $\\mathrm{ML}^{2} \\mathrm{~T}^{-2}, \\theta_{2}-\\theta_{1}$ is temperature, Thus,<br>                 <br>                 $$<br>                 [k]=\\frac{\\mathrm{ML}^{2} \\mathrm{~T}^{-2} \\mathrm{~L}}{\\mathrm{~L}^{2} \\mathrm{KT}}=\\mathrm{MLT}^{-3} \\mathrm{~K}^{-1}<br>                 $$<br>                 <br>                 (d) $\\quad F=-\\eta A \\frac{v_{2}-v_{1}}{x_{2}-x_{1}}$<br>                 <br>                 or, $\\quad \\mathrm{MLT}^{-2}=[\\eta] \\mathrm{L}^{2} \\frac{\\mathrm{L} / \\mathrm{T}}{\\mathrm{L}}=[\\eta] \\frac{\\mathrm{L}^{2}}{\\mathrm{~T}}$<br>                 <br>                 or, $\\quad[\\eta]=\\mathrm{ML}^{-1} \\mathrm{~T}^{-1}$.<br>                 <br>                 2. Find the dimensional formulae of<br>                 <br>                 (a) the charge $Q$,<br>                 <br>                 (b) the potential $V$,<br>                 <br>                 (c) the capacitance $C$, and<br>                 <br>                 (d) the resistance $R$.<br>                 <br>                 Some of the equations containing these quantities are<br>                 <br>                 $$<br>                 Q=I t, U=V I t, Q=C V \\text { and } V=R I \\text {; }<br>                 $$<br>                 <br>                 where I denotes the electric current, $t$ is time and $U$ is energy.<br>                 <br>                 Solution : (a) $Q=I t . \\quad$ Hence, $[Q]=I T$.<br>                 <br>                 (b)<br>                 <br>                 $U=V I t$<br>                 <br>                 or, $\\mathrm{ML}^{2} \\mathrm{~T}^{-2}=[V] \\mathrm{IT}$<br>                 <br>                 or, $[V]=\\mathrm{ML}^{2} \\mathrm{I}^{-1} \\mathrm{~T}^{-3}$. How many ergs are equal to one joule?<br>                 <br>                 Solution : Dimensionally, Energy $=$ mass $\\times(\\text { velocity })^{2}$<br>                 <br>                 $$<br>                 =\\text { mass } \\times\\left(\\frac{\\text { length }}{\\text { time }}\\right)^{2}=\\mathrm{ML}^{2} \\mathrm{~T}^{-2}<br>                 $$<br>                 <br>                 Thus, 1 joule $=(1 \\mathrm{~kg})(1 \\mathrm{~m})^{2}(1 \\mathrm{~s})^{-2}$<br>                 <br>                 and $\\quad 1 \\mathrm{erg}=(1 \\mathrm{~g})(1 \\mathrm{~cm})^{2}(1 \\mathrm{~s})^{-2}$<br>                 <br>                 $$<br>                 \\begin{aligned}<br>                 \\frac{1 \\text { joule }}{1 \\mathrm{erg}} & =\\left(\\frac{1 \\mathrm{~kg}}{1 \\mathrm{~g}}\\right)\\left(\\frac{1 \\mathrm{~m}}{1 \\mathrm{~cm}}\\right)^{2}\\left(\\frac{1 \\mathrm{~s}}{1 \\mathrm{~s}}\\right)^{-2} \\\\<br>                 & =\\left(\\frac{1000 \\mathrm{~g}}{1 \\mathrm{~g}}\\right)\\left(\\frac{100 \\mathrm{~cm}}{1 \\mathrm{~cm}}\\right)^{2}=1000 \\times 10000=10^{7}<br>                 \\end{aligned}<br>                 $$<br>                 <br>                 So, $\\quad 1$ joule $=10^{7}$ erg.<br>                 <br>                 4. Young's modulus of steel is $19 \\times 10^{10} \\mathrm{~N} / \\mathrm{m}^{2}$. Express it in dyne $/ \\mathrm{cm}^{2}$. If velocity, time and force were chosen as basic quantities, find the dimensions of mass.<br>                 <br>                 Solution : Dimensionally, Force $=$ mass $\\times$ acceleration<br>                 <br>                 $$<br>                 =\\text { mass } \\times \\frac{\\text { velocity }}{\\text { time }}<br>                 $$<br>                 <br>                 or,<br>                 <br>                 $$<br>                 \\text { mass }=\\frac{\\text { force } \\times \\text { time }}{\\text { velocity }}<br>                 $$<br>                 <br>                 or,<br>                 <br>                 $$<br>                 [\\text { mass }]=F T V^{-1} \\text {. }<br>                 $$<br>                 <br>                 6. Test dimensionally if the equation $v^{2}=u^{2}2 a x$ may be correct.<br>                 <br>                 Solution : There are three terms in this equation $v^{2}, u^{2}$ and 2ax. The equation may be correct if the dimensions of these three terms are equal.<br>                 <br>                 $$<br>                 \\begin{aligned}<br>                 & {\\left[v^{2}\\right]=\\frac{L^{2}}{\\mathrm{~T}}=\\mathrm{L}^{2} \\mathrm{~T}^{-2} ;} \\\\<br>                 & {\\left[u^{2}\\right]=\\frac{\\mathrm{L}}{\\mathrm{T}}=\\mathrm{L}^{2} \\mathrm{~T}^{-2} ;}<br>                 \\end{aligned}<br>                 $$<br>                 <br>                 and<br>                 <br>                 $$<br>                 [2 a x]=[a][x]=\\frac{\\mathrm{L}}{\\mathrm{T}^{2}} \\quad \\mathrm{~L}=\\mathrm{L}^{2} \\mathrm{~T}^{-2}<br>                 $$<br>                 <br>                 Thus, the equation may be correct.<br>                 <br>                 7. The distance covered by a particle in time $t$ is given by $x=ab tc t^{2}d t^{3}$; find the dimensions of $a, b, c$ and $d$.<br>                 <br>                 Solution : The equation contains five terms. All of them should have the same dimensions. Since $[x]=$ length, each of the remaining four must have the dimension of length.<br>                 <br>                 Thus, $\\quad[a]=$ length $=\\mathrm{L}$<br>                 <br>                 $$<br>                 \\begin{aligned}<br>                 {[b t] } & =\\mathrm{L}, & & \\text { or, }[b]=\\mathrm{LT}^{-1} \\\\<br>                 {\\left[c t^{2}\\right] } & =\\mathrm{L}, & & \\text { or, }[c]=\\mathrm{LT}^{-2} \\\\<br>                 \\text { and } \\quad\\left[d t^{3}\\right] & =\\mathrm{L}, & & \\text { or, }[d]=\\mathrm{LT}^{-3} .<br>                 \\end{aligned}<br>                 $$<br>                 <br>                 8. If the centripetal force is of the form $m^{a} v^{b} r^{c}$, find the values of $a, b$ and $c$.<br>                 <br>                 Solution : Dimensionally,<br>                 <br>                 $$<br>                 \\begin{aligned}<br>                 \\text { Force } & =(\\text { Mass })^{a} \\times(\\text { velocity })^{b} \\times(\\text { length })^{c} \\\\<br>                 \\text { or, } \\quad \\mathrm{MLT}^{-2} & =\\mathrm{M}^{a}\\left(\\mathrm{~L}^{b} \\mathrm{~T}^{-b}\\right) \\mathrm{L}^{c}=\\mathrm{M}^{a} \\mathrm{~L}^{bc} \\mathrm{~T}^{-b}<br>                 \\end{aligned}<br>                 $$<br>                 <br>                 Equating the exponents of similar quantities,<br>                 <br>                 $$<br>                 \\begin{aligned}<br>                 a & =1, bc=1,-b=-2 \\\\<br>                 \\text { or, } \\quad a & =1, b=2, c=-1 \\quad \\text { or, } F=\\frac{m v^{2}}{r} .<br>                 \\end{aligned}<br>                 $$<br>                 <br>                 9. When a solid sphere moves through a liquid, the liquid opposes the motion with a force $F$. The magnitude of $F$ depends on the coefficient of viscosity $\\eta$ of the liquid, the radius $r$ of the sphere and the speed $v$ of the sphere.<br>                 Assuming that $F$ is proportional to different powers of these quantities, guess a formula for $F$ using the method of dimensions.<br>                 <br>                 Solution : Suppose the formula is $F=k \\eta^{a} r^{b} v^{c}$.<br>                 <br>                 Then, $\\quad M L T^{-2}=\\left[\\mathrm{ML}^{-1} \\mathrm{~T}^{-1}\\right]^{a} \\mathrm{~L}^{b} \\frac{\\mathrm{L}^{c}}{\\mathrm{~T}}$<br>                 <br>                 $$<br>                 =\\mathbf{M}^{a} \\mathrm{~L}^{-abc} \\mathbf{T}^{-a-c}<br>                 $$<br>                 <br>                 Equating the exponents of $\\mathrm{M}, \\mathrm{L}$ and $\\mathrm{T}$ from both sides,<br>                 <br>                 $$<br>                 \\begin{aligned}<br>                 a & =1 \\\\<br>                 -abc & =1 \\\\<br>                 -a-c & =-2<br>                 \\end{aligned}<br>                 $$<br>                 <br>                 Solving these, $a=1, b=1$, and $c=1$.<br>                 <br>                 Thus, the formula for $F$ is $F=k \\eta r v$.<br>                 <br>                 10.
